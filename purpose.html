Purpose

Teal Trails started with a few girls I met in an orphanage, girls who'd tested positive for HPV and cervical cancer, and had been isolated because of it. When I asked what would happen to them, the answer I got was, "What can you even do in such situations?" That stayed with me.

The more I looked into it, the more the problem widened. What began as a question about cervical cancer led me to deeper, everyday struggles around menstrual health in my own community; labour workers using pieces of cloth as pads, and countless silences around periods that nobody spoke about. Women's health, I realized, wasn't lacking in just one place, it was lacking in so many places where systems had gone unquestioned.

That's the root of Teal Trails, real girls and women whose stories deserved more than "what can you even do."

Importance

Only 3.9% of rural women aged 30–49 in Telangana have ever been screened for cervical cancer, a disease that's almost entirely preventable.

Rural women across India are 64% more likely than urban women to rely on unhygienic menstrual practices, often simply because sanitary products and private toilets aren't within reach.

Over half of women aged 15–49 in Telangana are anemic, prevalence rose from 53.1% to 57.6% between NFHS-4 and NFHS-5.
